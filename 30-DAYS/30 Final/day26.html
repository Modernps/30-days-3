Cyber-Attacks: II

Learn About Ransomware: How It Works and How to Avoid Infection

Ransomware is a type of malicious software (malware) that encrypts a victim’s files or locks them out of their devices, demanding a ransom payment (typically in cryptocurrency) to restore access. This malware is often delivered through phishing emails, malicious downloads, or compromised websites. Once infected, the victim is given an ultimatum: pay the ransom or lose access to the encrypted data.

Real-Life Example: The WannaCry Ransomware Attack

In 2017, the WannaCry ransomware attack targeted hundreds of thousands of computers worldwide, exploiting a vulnerability in older versions of Windows. The attack encrypted data and demanded ransom payments in Bitcoin. The most significant impact was felt by hospitals in the UK, where patient care was disrupted as systems went offline, leading to delayed surgeries and other medical procedures. The attack highlighted the devastating effects of ransomware, particularly on critical infrastructure, and emphasized the need for regular system updates and backups to avoid such catastrophic consequences.

Risks of Not Taking Ransomware Seriously

There is a reason why most business peoples and firms fear ransomware:

  • Data Loss: If infected, you may permanently lose access to personal files, business documents, or sensitive data unless you pay the ransom (which doesn’t guarantee recovery).

  • Financial Costs: Paying the ransom is costly, and there is no assurance that the attackers will unlock your data or refrain from demanding more money in the future.

  • Reputation Damage: For businesses, a ransomware attack can damage customer trust and brand reputation, especially if sensitive data is exposed or business operations are paralyzed.

How to Avoid Ransomware Infection

Ransomware often spreads through malicious email attachments or infected files downloaded from untrusted websites.

  • Be cautious of unexpected emails: If you receive an email with an attachment or link from an unknown sender, do not open it.

  • Verify sources: To check for available macOS updates, go to System Settings (or System Preferences in older versions), select Software Update, and macOS will automatically check for updates.

  • Download only from trusted websites: Stick to official websites or trusted platforms when downloading software or files.

Antivirus and anti-malware software can help detect and block ransomware before it infects your system.

  • Install trusted antivirus software (e.g., Norton, Bitdefender, or Malwarebytes).

  • Ensure real-time protection is enabled to block ransomware before it runs.

  • Set up automatic updates to keep your security software up to date against the latest threats.

Many ransomware attacks exploit known vulnerabilities in outdated systems, like the case with WannaCry.

  • Enable automatic updates on your operating system to ensure that security patches are installed as soon as they’re released.

  • Regularly check for updates for your operating system, browser, and software.

Having a secure, up-to-date backup can save you in the event of a ransomware attack. If your data is encrypted, you can restore it from a clean backup without paying the ransom.

  • Use an external hard drive or a cloud-based service to back up your important files regularly.

  • Ensure backups are stored in a location that ransomware cannot reach (e.g., offline or in the cloud with proper encryption).

Ransomware attackers often gain access to systems through poorly secured RDP connections.

  • Disable RDP on your system if you do not use it for remote access.

  • If you must use RDP, ensure it’s secured with a strong password and multi-factor authentication, and restrict access to trusted IP addresses only.
