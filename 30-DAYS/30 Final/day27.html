Cyber-Attacks: III

Familiarize Yourself with Password Attacks: Brute Force, Dictionary Attacks, and How to Defend Against Them

What are Password Attacks

Password attacks are attempts by cybercriminals to gain unauthorized access to your accounts or systems by cracking or guessing your passwords. Two common types of password attacks are brute force and dictionary attacks:

  • Brute Force Attack: In a brute force attack, the attacker systematically tries every possible combination of characters until they guess the correct password.

  • Dictionary Attack: A dictionary attack involves using a list of common passwords or dictionary words to attempt to break into an account. This method is faster than brute force because it uses precompiled lists of known, frequently used passwords.

Real-Life Example: The Yahoo Data Breach

One of the largest data breaches in history occurred in 2013 and affected Yahoo. Over 3 billion accounts were compromised, primarily due to weak passwords that attackers exploited through password-guessing techniques, including brute force and dictionary attacks. The breach exposed sensitive information such as names, phone numbers, and hashed passwords, ultimately causing significant financial and reputational damage to Yahoo. It highlighted the dangers of using weak passwords and reusing them across multiple platforms.

Risks of Not Defending Against Password Attacks


  • Account Compromise: Weak or reused passwords make it easier for attackers to take over your accounts, giving them access to everything from your emails to your bank accounts.

  • Identity Theft: Once attackers have your password, they can steal your personal information, apply for loans in your name, or commit other fraudulent activities.

  • Financial Loss: Whether through personal accounts or business systems, attackers can steal funds, drain bank accounts, or cause significant financial damage.

  • Reputation Damage: For businesses, a breach caused by weak passwords can erode customer trust and damage your brand’s reputation.

How to Defend Against Password Attacks

Weak passwords are easy to crack through both brute force and dictionary attacks. By using strong, complex passwords, you make it harder for attackers to guess your credentials.


Step:

  • Create passwords with at least 12-16 characters, including a mix of uppercase and lowercase letters, numbers, and special characters.

  • Avoid common words or easily guessable information like birthdates, names, or simple number sequences.

  • Don’t reuse passwords across multiple sites or accounts.

MFA adds an additional layer of security by requiring more than just a password to access your accounts. Even if an attacker obtains your password, they will still need the second authentication factor, such as a code sent to your phone.

A password manager can generate and store strong, unique passwords for all your accounts, ensuring that each account has a different, secure password without requiring you to remember them all.


Step:

  • Download a reputable password manager such as LastPass, Bitwarden, or 1Password.

  • Let the manager create strong, random passwords for each of your accounts.

  • Store all your credentials securely and use the manager’s auto-fill feature to simplify the login process.

By limiting the number of incorrect login attempts on your accounts, you can prevent brute force attacks that rely on systematically guessing passwords.


Steps:

  • Use services that automatically lock accounts after a certain number of failed login attempts.

  • If you run a website or service, implement rate-limiting features that temporarily block IP addresses after multiple incorrect login attempts.

  • Set up alerts to notify you of any unusual or failed login attempts on your accounts.

By limiting the number of incorrect login attempts on your accounts, you can prevent brute force attacks that rely on systematically guessing passwords.


Steps:

  • For personal accounts, choose services that use CAPTCHA challenges during the login process.

  • If you manage a website, enable CAPTCHA for all login, registration, and password recovery forms to reduce automated attacks.

Changing your passwords regularly can help minimize the risk if an attacker has already compromised one of your accounts. Frequent updates ensure that even if a password is exposed, the window of opportunity for an attacker is short.


Steps:

  • Change your passwords every 3 to 6 months, especially for critical accounts like banking, email, or any sensitive services.

  • Set reminders or use your password manager to alert you when it’s time to update your passwords.
